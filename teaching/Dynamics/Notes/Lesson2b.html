Curvilinear motion: Normal-Tangential Coordinates

Consider a particle that moves a long a curved path. At any given point we identify a unit vector called \(\hat{e}_t\) that points in the direction tangent to the path and in the direction of motion. The velocity vector can therefore be expressed as: \begin{align} \vec{v}(t)=v(t)\hat{e}_t \end{align} where \(v(t)\) is the speed of the particle as a function of time. Note that while the magnitude of \(\hat{e}_t\) is one by definition, its direction changes as the particle moves along the path. We won't derive this here but the acceleration in this coordinate system is expressed as \begin{align} \vec{a}=\frac{dv}{dt}\hat{e}_t+\frac{v^2}{\rho}\hat{e}_n \end{align} where \(\hat{e}_n\) is a unit vector pointing into the center of curvature of the path and \(\rho\) is the radius of curvature of the path. It will sometimes be convenient to represent the acceleration in terms of its scalar components \begin{align*} a_t=\frac{dv}{dt}\,,\qquad a_n=\frac{v^2}{\rho} \end{align*} such that \begin{align} \vec{a}=a_t\hat{e}_t+a_n\hat{e}_n \end{align} In summary, the tangential component of the acceleration \(a_t\) changes the speed of the particle, while the normal component \(a_n\) results in a change in direction.
